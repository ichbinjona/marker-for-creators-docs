# Documentary / Reportage Workflow

A documentary isn't filmed in a day. It's interviews across weeks, location shoots across cities, B-roll accumulated over months. The challenge isn't the filming - it's remembering what you have.

Six months into production, can you find the exact moment your subject talked about their childhood? Can your editor locate that one establishing shot of the factory at golden hour? Without a system, the answer is "maybe, after an hour of searching."

With Marker, every piece of footage is annotated the moment it's captured.

## Before Each Shoot Day

### Set Up Your Markers

Create a marker preset called "Documentary":

| Button | Color | Name | Use |
|--------|-------|------|-----|
| 1 | Red | Key Moment | The core of the story - revelations, turning points, decisive statements |
| 2 | Green | Good Take | A solid section worth using |
| 3 | Blue | B-Roll | Location beauty, detail, atmosphere, insert shots |
| 4 | Yellow | Note | Technical note, continuity issue, or idea for later |

### Set Up Your Timelines

For interview days:
- **Interview** - The main conversation
- **Reaction Shots** - Interviewer nods, listening shots
- **B-Roll** - Cutaway material filmed during setup or breaks

For location days:
- **Wide Establishing** - Big landscape and location shots
- **Detail / Insert** - Close-ups of objects, textures, hands
- **Action** - People doing things, processes, movement

## During an Interview

Interviews are where notes shine. Your subject says something powerful - you need to remember not just when, but what.

1. Start a **Time of Day** session
2. Set markers during the conversation:

| When | Tap |
|------|-----|
| Subject says something revelatory or emotional | **Key Moment** (Red) + add note |
| Strong answer, usable section | **Good Take** (Green) |
| Subject mentions something you need B-roll for | **Note** (Yellow) + add note: "Need insert of old photographs" |
| Technical issue or re-take needed | **Note** (Yellow) + add note: "Audio clip - reshoot" |

3. Use **note-to-next-marker** heavily: Before a new question, type the topic in the Note Bar and send right (→). Then mark when the answer starts. Your markers now have context: "Childhood in Berlin", "First company failure", "Relationship with father."

::: tip
Documentary interviews benefit most from detailed notes. Even a few words per marker - "tears up talking about mother" - save enormous amounts of time months later when you're in the edit and can't remember which of 20 interviews had that moment.
:::

## During Location Shoots

Location shoots are about collecting material. You might not know what you need yet, but you know you'll need it later.

1. Mark every shot you think is usable - even generously
2. Use notes to describe the shot: "Factory floor wide, steam, golden light" or "Hands sorting coffee beans close-up"
3. Tag B-Roll timeline clips for different visual categories

## Over the Course of Production

As your project spans weeks or months:

- Each shoot day generates a separate session
- Sessions are searchable in the Companion App by date and name
- Marker notes from every session are visible in the session panel
- Convert between editing softwares if your project moves from one to another

### Merging Sessions

In the Companion App, you can **merge multiple sessions** into one. This is especially powerful for documentaries: if you use the same timeline presets across shoot days (e.g., "Interview", "B-Roll", "Wide Establishing"), merging combines all clips from matching timelines into a single unified timeline.

This means you can shoot across 10 days over a period of months, merge all sessions, and have one coherent project with all your footage organized into the timelines you defined.

::: info

:::

## In the Edit

Your editor opens the Companion and has a bird's-eye view of the entire production:

- 15 interview sessions, each with timestamped, annotated markers
- Location B-roll sessions with descriptions of every shot
- Red markers across all sessions = the story's turning points

They can build the narrative by:
1. Scanning Key Moment (Red) markers across all interviews for the story's arc
2. Finding specific topics via marker notes
3. Pulling B-roll from location sessions matched by description
4. Creating rough cuts from Good Take (Green) sections

## Why This Matters

A feature documentary might have 100-300 hours of raw footage. Without annotation, your editor is working from memory and timecoded paper notes. With Marker, every session is a searchable, annotated document. The footage tells its own story before the edit even begins.
